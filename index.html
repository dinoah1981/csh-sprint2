1. Why we're redesigning the high school

Our mission has not changed: every CSH student to economic freedom by 25. The world we're preparing students for has. In an AI-shaped economy, the traditional all-day academic model — built when knowledge itself was scarce and valuable — no longer hits the target. Knowledge is still the foundation. But what students can do, build, and demonstrate is what will determine their value in the economy and the depth of their lives. If the mission is the same, the school has to change.

01

Mornings are for Knowing

Five core classes — English, Math, Science, History, CS — meet three times a week for an hour each. That's the academic foundation, focused and protected.

02

Afternoons are for Doing

Hands-on experiences in three categories: academic-applied integration (engineering, lab science, app-building, research writing), life skills (cooking, finance, civic engagement), and human experience & enrichment (arts, music, NYC culture). Every experience ends in one concrete deliverable.

03

Same mission, new path

Economic freedom by 25 still drives every choice. But the route now runs through what students have built and demonstrated — not just what they've absorbed. This pre-read is the design we're testing in Sprint 2.

External evidence

The Burning Glass Institute & aiEDU, "Which Skills Matter Now? A Data-Driven Framework for K–12 in the Age of AI" (February 2026). Analyzing AI's impact on 1,000 workforce skills and 140 high-school learning objectives, the report finds that roughly 70% of K–12 learning objectives will need to be taught differently in an AI-shaped economy. Three of its core themes line up directly with this redesign: skills with high automation exposure now demand deeper conceptual understanding (not less); every discipline contains skills that need new instructional approaches alongside skills that remain foundational; and traditional assessment must shift toward evaluating student thinking, reasoning, and judgment — not the polished outputs AI can generate. → burningglassinstitute.org/research/whichskillsmatternow

2. Core Competencies

Six domains. Domains 1–5 are about getting to economic freedom. Domain 6 is what economic freedom lets you enjoy. Green-shaded competencies (★) are the most essential — the ones every graduate must demonstrate. Unshaded competencies are still goals, but live behind the essentials in priority. Each domain is paired with the SCORE value it most depends on.

★ Essential — every graduate must demonstrate this.    ○ Aspirational — still a goal, but second-tier priority.
1

Career Readiness

SCORE: Excellence, Consistency

Students should graduate with the skills, habits, and reference points needed to enter the world of work with confidence. This domain is not only about technical skill; it is about experiencing real or workplace-analog settings so that graduates can say, 'I have done something like this before,' and know how to approach unfamiliar work challenges.

  • Communicates clearly in professional settings, orally and in writing
  • Completes authentic work experiences such as internships, client projects, or career related programs.
  • Demonstrates professional habits, including punctuality, reliability, preparation, follow-through, and communication
  • Uses feedback to improve performance and meet clear standards
  • Builds professional networks and manages their brand socially and digitally (resume, linkedin, finding mentors, maintaining relationships).
  • Collaborates effectively with peers, supervisors, and clients
  • Develops significant domain-specific knowledge, skill and experience in at least one career field.
  • Develops the confidence to enter unfamiliar work settings and "read" the culture.
  • Navigates common workplace expectations, norms, and responsibilities
2

Learning and Reasoning about the World

SCORE: Excellence

Students need both knowledge and the ability to use it. Reasoning cannot happen without facts, concepts, and disciplinary knowledge, and knowledge matters most when students can apply it to explain, analyze, interpret, and solve problems.

  • Applies disciplinary knowledge to solve problems and make sense of the world
  • Builds a strong foundation of knowledge in mathematics, science, history, literature, economics, civics, and science and technology, and an understanding of the current state of those fields. (Well enough to gain admission to and engage confidentally in college)
  • Interprets and constructs clear, evidence-based explanations and arguments
  • Reads and accurately interprets text.
  • Synthesizes information from multiple sources and perspectives
  • Uses historical and social knowledge to interpret the past and present
  • Uses mathematical reasoning to analyze quantitative problems and real situations
  • Uses philosophical, rhetorical, and analytical tools to examine ideas, texts, and claims
3

Agency, Problem Solving, and Perseverance

SCORE: Resilience

Students should not leave school waiting for directions. They should see learning as a tool for solving problems, develop the habit of taking initiative, and build the persistence to keep working when a solution is not immediately obvious. This matters for academic life, work, entrepreneurship, and a rapidly changing economy.

  • Learns new tools (including AI), resources, and even bureaucratic systems in service of solving real problems.
  • Persists through difficulty, ambiguity, and setbacks
  • Takes initiative to solve problemsin academic, professional, and personal contexts instead of waiting passively for instructions
  • Applies entrepreneurial thinking to opportunities, needs, and constraints
  • Breaks complex challenges into manageable steps
  • Generates, tests, and revises solutions through iteration
4

Caring for Self and Others

SCORE: Ownership, Consistency

A healthy life requires more than academic or economic success. Students should leave high school with the habits, knowledge, and relationships needed to care for their physical, emotional, and financial well-being, and to participate meaningfully in communities that enrich and sustain life.

  • Maintains habits that support physical health and long-term well-being
  • Practices basic financial habits and decision-making for adult life
  • Builds and sustains respectful, supportive relationships
  • Contributes to families, teams, and communities with care and responsibility
  • Develops and maintains a sense of belonging within a network or community
  • Recognizes, manages, and communicates about emotional needs in healthy ways
  • Understands how to seek help, access resources, and navigate common life systems
  • Understands that connection, mutual support, and community are part of a healthy life
5

Exploring Identity and Purpose

SCORE: Self Awareness

Students do not need to have a final answer to 'Who am I?' by age eighteen. They do need tools, language, and habits for exploring identity, joy, purpose, and motivation over time. This domain is about beginning a lifelong process of self-understanding rather than finishing it.

  • Begins to articulate values, commitments, and sources of purpose
  • Explore how their identity intersects with professional, economic and social success.
  • Develops habits of reflection that can continue beyond high school
  • Examines which parts of identity feel central, meaningful, or personally powerful
  • Explores how dimensions such as culture, ethnicity, language, geography, gender, and sexuality may or may not matter in their self-understanding
  • Reflects on the identities, affiliations, and experiences that shape who they are
  • Reflects on what motivates effort, commitment, and ambition
6

Enjoying and Enriching Life

SCORE: Self Awareness

Some experiences are worth cultivating because they enlarge human life. Exposure to art, music, literature, nature, and the richness of the city can deepen joy, curiosity, appreciation, and a sense of what makes life worth living. This domain could remain a distinct sixth domain or be woven into the five core domains.

  • Develops interests, hobbies, and practices that create joy outside of work
  • Appreciates art, music, literature, sports and other forms of human expression
  • Builds habits of curiosity and participation in the cultural life around them
  • Experiences nature as a source of beauty, reflection, and renewal
  • Explores New York City as a dynamic cultural and civic environment
  • Explores what brings joy, meaning, and fulfillment
  • Recognizes that pleasure, wonder, and aesthetic appreciation are part of a full life

3. Schedule Options

Four structures were considered. The morning is always academic core (classes 1–5). The afternoon is always experience blocks. Each option except Alternate Day was paired with two teacher prep distributions — Option 1 distributes preps across multiple days, Option 2 concentrates them on a single day so a department can arrive at 10:30 once a week and do its meeting in that protected morning. We chose 2-2-3 with Morning Advisory.

5. Design Sprint 2 — What We Will Discuss

The four sections above are the pre-read. The questions below are what we will work through together in the room. Come with your reactions to the materials, but also with the people we will need to convince and the practical realities of our current staff in mind.

AM

Reacting to the design

  1. What are your questions and reactions to the competencies?
  2. What are your questions and reactions to the schedule design?
  3. What are your questions and reactions to the notional list of experiences?
  4. What are the (initial) design parameters of a strong afternoon experience?
  5. When thinking about implementing this with our current staff, what are the opportunities (exciting things, upsides) and the likely challenges (things that will be hard for some or all)?
PM

Moving toward implementation

  1. What will we pilot, and when?
  2. What are the change management and staff engagement strategies and timeline?
  3. What are the key design next steps to have a full gameplan for next year?
