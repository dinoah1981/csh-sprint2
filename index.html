CSH High School Redesign

Why we are redesigning the high school, the competencies every graduate must hit, the schedule we chose, and the badge-based graduation requirements that fill the afternoon blocks with real experiences.

Why we're redesigning the high school

Our mission has not changed: every CSH student to economic freedom by 25. The world we're preparing students for has. In an AI-shaped economy, the traditional all-day academic model — built when knowledge itself was scarce and valuable — no longer hits the target. Knowledge is still the foundation. But what students can do, build, and demonstrate is what will determine their value in the economy and the depth of their lives. If the mission is the same, the school has to change.

01

Mornings are for Knowing

Five core classes — English, Math, Science, History, CS — meet three times a week for an hour each. That's the academic foundation, focused and protected.

02

Afternoons are for Doing

Hands-on experiences in three categories: academic-applied integration (engineering, lab science, app-building, research writing), life skills (cooking, finance, civic engagement), and human experience & enrichment (arts, music, NYC culture). Every experience ends in one concrete deliverable.

03

Same mission, new path

Economic freedom by 25 still drives every choice. But the route now runs through what students have built and demonstrated — not just what they've absorbed. This pre-read is the design we're testing in Sprint 2.

External evidence

The Burning Glass Institute & aiEDU, "Which Skills Matter Now? A Data-Driven Framework for K–12 in the Age of AI" (February 2026). Analyzing AI's impact on 1,000 workforce skills and 140 high-school learning objectives, the report finds that roughly 70% of K–12 learning objectives will need to be taught differently in an AI-shaped economy. Three of its core themes line up directly with this redesign: skills with high automation exposure now demand deeper conceptual understanding (not less); every discipline contains skills that need new instructional approaches alongside skills that remain foundational; and traditional assessment must shift toward evaluating student thinking, reasoning, and judgment — not the polished outputs AI can generate. → burningglassinstitute.org/research/whichskillsmatternow

Core Competencies

Five domains, all in service of the same destination: economic freedom. These are the revised lists coming out of Design Sprint 2, trimmed from the earlier six-domain draft. The old sixth domain, Enjoying and Enriching Life, has not disappeared — it is woven into Caring for Self and Others and Exploring Identity and Purpose, and it shows up concretely as required arts, movement, and enrichment badges in the graduation requirements (see the Requirements tab). The essential-vs.-aspirational split is gone: every competency below (✓) is a commitment every graduate must demonstrate. Each domain is paired with the SCORE value it most depends on.

1

Career Readiness

SCORE: Excellence, Consistency

Students should graduate with the skills, habits, and reference points needed to enter the world of work with confidence. This domain is not only about technical skill; it is about experiencing real or workplace-analog settings so that graduates can say, 'I have done something like this before,' and know how to approach unfamiliar work challenges.

  • Communicates clearly in professional settings, orally and in writing
  • Collaborates effectively with peers, supervisors, and clients, building a network of mentors and contacts along the way
  • Uses feedback to improve performance and meet clear standards
  • Demonstrates professional habits by correctly interpreting standards, including punctuality, dress code, communication, preparation, and follow-through
  • Manages their brand socially and digitally (resume, LinkedIn, finding mentors, maintaining relationships)
  • Develops significant domain-specific knowledge, skill, and experience in at least one career field
2

Learning and Reasoning about the World

SCORE: Excellence

Students need both knowledge and the ability to use it. Reasoning cannot happen without facts, concepts, and disciplinary knowledge, and knowledge matters most when students can apply it to explain, analyze, interpret, and solve problems.

  • Builds a strong foundation of knowledge in mathematics, science, history, literature, economics, civics, and science and technology, and an understanding of the current state of those fields — well enough to gain admission to and engage confidently in college
  • Draws on knowledge from across different sources and even classes to explain phenomena in the world and to build schema
  • Interprets and constructs clear, evidence-based explanations and arguments
  • Reads and accurately interprets text and other media
3

Agency, Problem Solving, and Perseverance

SCORE: Resilience

Students should not leave school waiting for directions. They should see learning as a tool for solving problems, develop the habit of taking initiative, and build the persistence to keep working when a solution is not immediately obvious. This matters for academic life, work, entrepreneurship, and a rapidly changing economy.

  • Takes initiative to solve problems in academic, professional, and personal contexts instead of waiting passively for instructions
  • Persists through difficulty, ambiguity, and setbacks
  • Knows how to break complex challenges into manageable steps
  • Learns new tools (including AI), resources, and even bureaucratic systems in service of solving real problems
  • Generates, tests, and revises solutions through iteration
4

Caring for Self and Others

SCORE: Ownership, Consistency

A healthy life requires more than academic or economic success. Students should leave high school with the habits, knowledge, and relationships needed to care for their physical, emotional, and financial well-being, and to participate meaningfully in communities that enrich and sustain life.

  • Recognizes, manages, and communicates about emotional needs in healthy ways
  • Maintains habits that support physical health and long-term well-being
  • Practices basic financial habits and decision-making for adult life
  • Builds and sustains respectful, supportive relationships
  • Develops and maintains a sense of belonging within community, and actively contributes to that community
  • Understands how to seek help, access resources, and navigate common life systems
5

Exploring Identity and Purpose

SCORE: Self Awareness

Students do not need to have a final answer to 'Who am I?' by age eighteen. They do need tools, language, and habits for exploring identity, joy, purpose, and motivation over time. This domain is about beginning a lifelong process of self-understanding rather than finishing it.

  • Develops habits of reflection that can continue beyond high school, including on elements of identity such as personality traits, culture, ethnicity, class, gender, sexuality, and community affiliations
  • Begins to articulate values, commitments, and sources of purpose and meaning
  • Explores what makes them happy and gives them pleasure, including arts, music, travel, sports, hobbies, and civic engagement
  • Explores how their identity intersects with professional, economic, and social success

Schedule Options

Four structures were considered. The morning is always academic core (classes 1–5). The afternoon is always experience blocks. Each option except Alternate Day was paired with two teacher prep distributions — Option 1 distributes preps across multiple days, Option 2 concentrates them on a single day so a department can arrive at 10:30 once a week and do its meeting in that protected morning. Graduation Requirements — A Badging System

The afternoon is not electives bolted onto a school day — it is where a large share of our graduation requirements live. Instead of traditional seat-time distribution requirements, students earn badges. A badge is earned by completing an experience and its single concrete deliverable (pass/fail). Each badge stamps one credit on the student's passport, and the passport — not a transcript of seat hours — is the record of what a graduate has actually done. Badging is our updated way of describing distribution requirements: the structure guarantees breadth (every graduate reads, builds, manages money, makes art, explores careers, and leads something real), while the menus inside each badge preserve genuine student choice. Every badge below sits in one of three tiers: badges everyone earns, badges where you pick the flavor, and open slots that are totally yours.
